The challenge of losing your life

35 For whoever would save his life will lose it, but whoever loses his life for my sake and the gospel's will save it. 36 For what does it profit a man to gain the whole world and forfeit his soul? 37 For what can a man give in return for his soul?

Mark 8:35-37 ESV

This passage and the parallel passages in Matthew 16 and Luke 9 are some of the most amazing words recorded in the Gospels. I vividly remember the first time that I heard a teaching on this passage over 20 years ago. I could not get it out of my head. I could not believe that Jesus was making such an ambitious call of what it means to follow Him. There is no part of my life that He is not asking for, and I was not entirely sure that was acceptable. I do not remember exactly how I resolved the tension at that time. I do know that interacting with God about the passage was an imporatant part of processing it. I also know that I had a different relationship with God after that, and I am thankful for it. After reading this again, I want to briefly examine three questions about the passage.

What does it mean to lose your life?

This has to be the core question we address when examining this passage. I think the answer is that we must be wholly devoted to God. We are no longer our own, we were bought with a price (1 Cor 6:20) and we must act consistent with this reality. Jesus wants us to examine our lives and come to Him. Just before this passage He is speaking to the disciples about His death and resurrection. He was here to save humanity, so the first way to lose your life is to believe what Jesus says about His. Jesus claims that we can know God and have eternal life with Him multiple times throughout the accounts of His life. All that is necessary is that we open the door for him to come into our lives (Rev 3:20). That choice will begin the process of changing my life from one of trying to save it, to one of trying to lose it.

How does this impact my daily life?

I can accept that Jesus is the owner of my time, but that does not mean that it works out well in my daily life. This is the second part of the challenge of this passage. It is not enough that I can say that Jesus death on the cross has transformed my future. I must be able to say that every action I take, every choice that I make, and all the thoughts in my head are submitted to Christ. There must be a radical change in the focus of our lives if we are to meet the challenge laid out in verse 35. Jesus addresses this in verses 36 and 37. We are either going to [try to] gain the whole world, or we are going to gain God by losing the world. I can either follow what I think is right, or I can follow what God says is right. This is one of the places in my life where I have seen the largest gap between knowing and doing. I will often know what it looks like to lose my life. I have a difficult time making the choice to do the right thing.

What happens if I ignore this?

Jesus tells us right after what I have quoted in Mark:

38 For whoever is ashamed of me and of my words in this adulterous and sinful generation, of him will the Son of Man also be ashamed when he comes in the glory of his Father with the holy angels.

This sounds extreme. I was just writing about how choosing to follow Jesus will change my eternity, and here Jesus is saying that He will be ashamed of me if I do not "lose my life". I do not believe that we need to be worried about eternity. There are plenty of places in God's word that we can see our eternal security is bought by Christ's death. What we miss out on instead is the great rewards that come from chosing to give my life to God, rather than following my own path. He has laid out plans for me (Jer 29:10), and will not forsake me (Joshua 1:5). A simple illustration that I see play out in my own home is when that I am often happier when I have been spending time relating with my family, than I am when I pursue my own interests. When I keep relying on Jesus' death on the cross to be my guidance in all situations, then I see changes in how I make choices.

Conclusions?

The best conclusion I have is that it is worth being challenged by God's word, and approaching Him with what you find there. He will meet you and help you see more of who He is. That is what happened to me with this passage, and my life is better for it.
